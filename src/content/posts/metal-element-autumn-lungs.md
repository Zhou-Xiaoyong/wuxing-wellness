**Metal** is the element of autumn — clarity, structure and letting go. In five-element theory it is linked with the lungs and the large intestine, and with the emotion of grief. Metal types are precise, principled and a little reserved; they value order and clean lines.

This is a cultural wellness perspective, not medical advice. Wheezing, persistent cough or breathlessness needs a clinician.

## The Metal type at a glance

Metal people tend to be:

- **Precise and principled** — they like standards, fairness and clean systems.
- **Reserved but deep** — slow to open, loyal once they do.
- **Sensitive to loss** — grief is Metal's signature emotion.

When Metal is balanced, these traits become integrity and quiet strength. When autumn "dryness" gets in, the same energy shows up as a dry cough, dry skin, constipation and a low, held-in sadness.

| Sign | You? |

A dry throat in heating season is common. Several together is a cue to add moisture.

## Autumn habits for Metal balance

1. **Add moisture.** Soups, stews and steamed pear are traditional autumn foods.
2. **Breathe on purpose.** Slow belly breathing supports the lungs and settles grief.
3. **Let go.** Metal's lesson is release — declutter, finish loose ends, grieve cleanly.
4. **Cover the neck.** Classical advice says wind at the nape invites illness; a scarf is cheap medicine.

## Recipe: pear & snow-fungus soup (Metal style)

A beloved autumn dessert-soup in Chinese homes — white, moist and gently sweet.

**Ingredients**
- 1 dried snow fungus ( Tremella ), soaked and torn
- 2 pears, cored and cubed
- 6 dried red dates (pitted)
- 5 cups water
- A few goji berries

**Method**
1. Simmer snow fungus in water 40 minutes until gelatinous.
2. Add pears and dates; simmer 20 more minutes.
3. Stir in goji berries off heat.
4. Serve warm. Keeps in the fridge 2–3 days.

A moist, soothing food ritual for dry seasons — not a treatment.

## The lungs and "letting go"

In five-element theory the lungs govern not just breath but the intake of fresh energy and the release of what's stale — physically and emotionally. Autumn is the natural season to practice both.
