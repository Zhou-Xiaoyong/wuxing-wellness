If you've ever felt *wired but tired* in July — sleepless by midnight, snappy by noon, a low-grade hum of restlessness you can't name — Chinese medicine has a tidy explanation, and it starts with the weather.

Summer is the Fire season. In the five-element map, Fire rules the heart. And the heart, in this tradition, does more than pump: it's said to *house the spirit* (the *shen*). So when the world outside gets hot and bright and overstimulating, the job of the heart is to stay cool enough to hold you steady. When it can't, you feel it first as a busy mind, not a racing pulse.

Here's the practical, kitchen-level version of that idea — no philosophy degree required.

## The heart in five-element terms

Think of the five elements as five modes the body runs in. Spring is Wood (growth), autumn is Metal (harvest and letting go), winter is Water (rest and storage). Summer is Fire: expansion, heat, outward energy, big feelings. The organ paired with Fire is the heart; the emotion paired with it is joy — but the *too-much* version, the giddy, can't-sit-still kind.

The old texts, like the *Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon* (《黄帝内经》), are blunt about summer: it's the time to be open, active, and a little outward — but not to the point of scorching yourself. The goal is a heart that's bright without being frantic.

## What "too much Fire" actually feels like

This isn't a diagnosis. It's a pattern worth noticing, because the fixes are gentle and free:

| Sign | Feels like me |
| --- | --- |
| Trouble falling or staying asleep | ☐ |
| A flushed face, especially in the evening | ☐ |
| Mouth ulcers or a sore tongue | ☐ |
| Restless, can't-sit-still energy | ☐ |
| Sharp hunger for stimulation (scrolling, snacks, noise) | ☐ |
| Light, broken sleep with vivid dreams | ☐ |

If most of those feel true in summer, your Fire is probably running a little hot. Good news: the levers are mostly on your plate.

## Bitter is the flavor of Fire — and it cools

Every element has a "correct" taste. Fire's is **bitter**. In Western terms that sounds like punishment; in Chinese food therapy it's the taste that *drains heat*. A little bitterness in summer is like cracking a window — it lets the steam out.

What that looks like in a real kitchen:

- **Bitter greens** — dandelion, endive, arugula, winter melon soup.
- **Mung bean** — the cooling summer staple, in tea or a light soup (try [mung bean & lotus-seed cooling tea](/recipes/mung-bean-lotus-tea)).
- **Green tea** over coffee in the afternoon.
- **A squeeze of lemon** on rich food to keep things light.

Notice what's *not* on the list: ice cream and iced lattes. Cold doesn't cool Fire — it just traps the heat deeper and, in this view, stresses the stomach. Room-temp or warm-cool beats icy every time.

## Cool the spirit, not just the body

Because the heart houses the spirit, summer self-care is as much about the nervous system as the thermostat. A few habits that read as very modern but map cleanly onto the old advice:

- **Protect the down-slope.** Big, bright days are for doing; evenings are for dimming. Screens last thing at night fight the exact cooling the heart wants.
- **Move early.** A morning walk in the cool beats a 6 p.m. run in the blast furnace.
- **A calm cup.** [Golden turmeric milk](/recipes/golden-turmeric-milk) or a chrysanthemum tea in the evening signals "day's over" to a body that's still humming.

## When to reach for the food, not the fix

This is a *tuning* practice, not medicine. The patterns above are worth playing with if you're simply feeling the season. But if you have chest pain, real palpitations, high blood pressure, or sleep that's been broken for weeks, that's a practitioner's job, not a tea's.

## A simple summer stack

If you want to try it for a week: swap one coffee for green tea, add a bitter green to one meal, take evening walks, and end the day with a warm (not hot, not iced) calming cup. Watch your sleep. Most people feel the difference faster than they expect — the body likes being met halfway.
