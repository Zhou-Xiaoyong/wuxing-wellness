One of the most practical ideas in five-element food theory is also the simplest: **food has five flavors, and each one nudges a different element.** Eat all five across a day and you cover the system. Lean too hard on one and the corresponding element gets overfed while others starve.

This is a cultural food perspective, not medical or nutritional advice.

## The flavor–element map

| Flavor | Element | Traditional role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sour (酸) | Wood | Contracts and gathers; supports liver |
| Bitter (苦) | Fire | Clears and cools; supports heart |
| Sweet (甘) | Earth | Nourishes and centers; supports digestion |
| Pungent (辛) | Metal | Disperses and warms; supports lungs |
| Salty (咸) | Water | Softens and stores; supports kidneys |

Note that "sweet" here means naturally sweet — grains, squash, yams — not refined sugar, which five-element practice would call an Earth overload.

## Quick self-check: which flavor are you missing?

| If you often feel… | You may need more… |
| --- | --- |
| Tense, irritable, stagnant | Sour (Wood) |
| Hot, restless, sleepless | Bitter (Fire) |
| Bloated, worried, low | Sweet (Earth, natural) |
| Congested, cold, low mood | Pungent (Metal) |
| Cold, fatigued, achy | Salty (Water, natural) |

This is a rough guide, not a rule. Most people just need a more even spread.

## How to use it without overthinking

You don't need a chart at every meal. A simple habit: look at your day and ask, *did I taste all five?* A plate of rice (sweet), greens with lemon (sour), a bitter green (bitter), scallion/ginger (pungent) and a salty broth covers the map in one bowl.

## Recipe: five-flavor balanced bowl

A single meal that hits all five — naturally.

**Ingredients**
- 1 cup cooked brown rice (sweet)
- 1 cup sautéed greens with lemon (sour)
- 1 cup bitter greens (chicory/radicchio), lightly cooked (bitter)
- Scallion + ginger, sliced thin (pungent)
- Miso broth splash (salty)
- Sesame seeds to finish

**Method**
1. Build the bowl: rice base, greens on top.
2. Scatter scallion, ginger and sesame.
3. Warm a little miso in water (don't boil it) and drizzle over.
4. Eat slowly. Notice the range of tastes.

Everyday food, not a cure — just a practical way to taste the whole system.

## The bigger point

Flavor is the easiest entry into five-element eating because it's something you already notice at every meal. Start there, and the element–organ map stops being abstract and becomes dinner.
