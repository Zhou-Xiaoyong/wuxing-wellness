Spring is the Wood season. In traditional Chinese wellness, spring's rising energy mirrors the liver's job: to keep everything moving. When the season and the organ line up, a few small habits go a long way.

This is a cultural and food-based perspective, not medical advice. If you have a diagnosed liver condition, follow your clinician's guidance.

## Why spring is "liver time"

The liver is described as the organ that smooths the flow of energy and emotion. Spring, with its bursts of growth, is when that energy wants to rise and expand. Eat and live in a way that supports the rise, and you feel lighter. Work against it, and tension builds.

## Self-check: is your liver energy "stuck"?

In five-element language, spring tension often reads as *liver qi stagnation*. See how many fit:

| Sign | You? |
| --- | --- |
| Irritable or short-tempered in spring | ☐ |
| Tight chest or sighing a lot | ☐ |
| Waking around 1–3 a.m. | ☐ |
| Tension headaches, especially at the sides | ☐ |
| Feeling "blah" despite enough sleep | ☐ |

A few ticks is common after a heavy winter. It's a nudge to lighten up — not a diagnosis.

## Food-first spring routine

- **More green, leafy vegetables.** Bitter and sour greens are the traditional Wood foods — think spinach, sprouts, celery, dandelion.
- **Less heavy, greasy food.** The liver prefers light, warm meals in spring.
- **A little sour.** Lemon in water or a few sour plums nudges liver energy in the traditional view.
- **Tender spring herbs.** Chives, cilantro and mint appear in many spring kitchens for a reason.

## Beyond food

- **Move outdoors.** A daily walk in fresh air is the simplest seasonal habit.
- **Stretch.** Wood rules tendons; gentle stretching relieves the tightness stress creates.
- **Watch the temper.** Irritability in spring is often read as rising liver energy — a sign to slow, not push harder.

## Recipe: warming chive & sprout stir-fry (Wood style)

A classic spring plate in Chinese home cooking — quick, green and lightly sour.

**Ingredients**
- 1 cup soybean or mung sprouts
- 1 small bunch garlic chives, cut in 2-inch pieces
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- Splash of rice vinegar
- Pinch of sea salt

**Method**
1. Warm sesame oil in a pan over medium heat.
2. Add chives and sprouts; stir 60–90 seconds — keep them crisp.
3. Off heat, add rice vinegar and salt; toss.
4. Serve warm as a side to rice or noodles.

This is everyday food, not a cure. It simply leans into the season's traditional flavors.

## You don't need a "cleanse"

In five-element thinking, spring care is mostly about *not blocking* the natural upward movement — through food, movement and a little patience. Skip extreme fasts; consistency beats intensity.
