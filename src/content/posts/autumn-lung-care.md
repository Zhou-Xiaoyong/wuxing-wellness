There's a particular kind of rough you feel in October. Not sick, exactly — just dry. Dry throat, dry lips that split, skin that drinks lotion and asks for more, a tickle of a cough that shows up at night. If you live somewhere with real seasons, you know the feeling.

Chinese medicine calls this *autumn dryness* (秋燥), and it has a tidy logic to it. Autumn is the Metal season. Metal rules the lungs — and the lungs, in this system, are the most "delicate" organ, the one that hates to be dry. When the air outside loses its moisture, the lungs are the first to notice.

Here's the version you can actually use.

## Why metal = lungs = autumn

The five elements aren't just poetry; they're a way of sorting the body's jobs by season and texture. Metal is the element of contraction — harvest, letting go, pulling inward as the year cools. The organ paired with it is the lungs, which "descend and moisten" — they're supposed to deliver fluid down through the body. When autumn dries the air, that descending moisture has less to work with, and you feel it in the obvious places: throat, nose, skin, lips.

The old texts, the *Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon* (《黄帝内经》), describe autumn as the time to "gather and settle" — to pull back from summer's outward burst and protect what's inside. Moisture is part of what you protect.

## The dryness checklist

Not a diagnosis — just a pattern worth naming so you can do something about it:

| Sign | Feels like me |
| --- | --- |
| Dry throat or a scratchy, night-time cough | ☐ |
| Lips that crack or peel | ☐ |
| Skin that suddenly feels tight and flaky | ☐ |
| A dry nose, or easy nosebleeds | ☐ |
| Dry eyes, worse than usual | ☐ |
| A low, lingering sadness you can't place | ☐ |

That last one isn't random. In five-element thinking, the emotion tied to Metal and the lungs is grief — the "letting go" feeling of the season. A dry, contracted body in autumn often rides alongside a tender, wistful mood. They're the same weather, inside and out.

## White foods moisten the lungs

Here's the elegant part: the color linked to Metal is **white**, and the foods that traditionally moisten the lungs tend to be white and soft. Coincidence? Maybe. But the grocery list writes itself:

- **Pears** — the poster child. Stewed or poached, never ice-cold.
- **Snow fungus (Tremella)** — a gelatinous fungus that turns silky as it simmers; the base of the classic [pear & snow-fungus soup](/recipes/pear-snow-fungus-soup).
- **Lily bulb (bai he)** — mild, starchy, quietly calming.
- **Sesame and honey** — a little healthy fat and sweetness to seal moisture in.
- **White fungus, tofu, lotus root** — all on the "moistening" side of the menu.

And the drink: a [chrysanthemum tea](/recipes/chrysanthemum-tea) takes the edge off the dry, hot-afternoon version of autumn, when the heat hasn't fully left.

## Habits that match the season

- **Ease off the cold and raw.** Summer's salads and iced drinks are Metal-season enemies. Warm, cooked, moist foods do the work.
- **Breathe on purpose.** The lungs love movement. A slow walk with real, full exhales is lung physical therapy.
- **Let something go.** Metal is the release season. One small purge — a drawer, a grudge, a habit — fits the body's rhythm better than you'd think.

## Food, not fix

This is tuning, not treatment. If you've got a real cough that won't quit, wheezing, or breathlessness, that's a clinician's lane. But for the ordinary October rough, the white-and-moistening grocery list plus a little warmth goes a long way.
