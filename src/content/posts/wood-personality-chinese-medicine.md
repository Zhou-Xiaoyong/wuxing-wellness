If you've ever met someone who treats life like a project with a deadline, you've probably met a **Wood** type. In Chinese medicine (中医, zhōng yī), Wood is the elemental energy of spring — the upward, outward push of a seed breaking soil. It's ambitious, visionary, and a little impatient. And it lives, more than any other element, in the **liver** (肝, gān) and **gallbladder** (胆, dǎn).

This article is about the Wood personality as Chinese medicine describes it — a way of reading temperament through the body's energies. It's a framework for self-reflection, not a diagnosis, and nothing here is medical advice.

## Wood and the liver: a two-thousand-year pairing

The link between Wood and the liver isn't poetic — it's foundational. The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon (《黄帝内经》, Huáng Dì Nèi Jīng), the cornerstone text of Chinese medicine, assigns each element an organ system and an emotion. Wood's organ is the liver; Wood's emotion is **anger** (怒, nù).

In this view the liver's job is to keep energy — *qi* (气, qì) — moving smoothly through the body. Think of it as the body's traffic controller. When qi flows, you feel decisive, energized, and clear. When it doesn't, the same Wood drive turns inward and becomes frustration, tightness, and that particular headache behind the eyes.

> A Wood person isn't "angry" by flaw. Their liver energy simply *wants to move*. Block the movement — a stalled plan, an unfair rule, a held tongue — and the pressure has to go somewhere.

## The signature pattern: liver qi stagnation

The phrase you'll hear most around Wood types is **liver qi stagnation** (肝气郁结, gān qì yù jié). It's the technical name for "stuck energy," and the symptoms read like a modern stress checklist:

- Irritability and a short fuse
- Tightness in the chest, shoulders, or rib sides
- Sighing more than usual (the body trying to move the stuck air)
- Waking around 1–3 a.m., the liver's hours in the traditional clock
- A churning stomach when something's unresolved

None of this means something is "wrong" with you. It means your Wood energy has hit a wall — and Wood hates walls.

## Why Wood types burn out differently

Because Wood is the element of *drive*, its burnout doesn't look like collapse. It looks like acceleration: more coffee, more lists, more push. The traditional view would say the liver is being asked to move energy it doesn't have, and the result is heat and tension rather than rest.

This is why Wood self-care so often fails when it's passive. A Wood person told to "just relax" usually can't. What actually helps is *movement with direction* — a walk with a purpose, a hard workout, a creative project that breaks the logjam. The liver wants flow, not stillness.

## Foods that lean into Wood energy

Chinese medicine frames food by temperature and action, not just calories. For a Wood type, the traditional supporters are:

- **Sour foods** — the Wood flavor. Lemon, vinegar, pickled greens, and young sprouts are said to "contract and gather," calming an over-driven system. The Five Flavors chapter of the Inner Canon ties Sour (酸, suān) to the liver.
- **Leafy greens** — chard, dandelion, bok choy. Bitter greens especially are used to "clear heat" when Wood has overheated.
- **Green things generally** — the Wood color. A plate of green is a small, daily nod to the element.
- **Rose buds** (玫瑰花, méi guī huā) — used to "move" stuck liver qi and lift the mood. Our [Rose & Citrus Tea for Wood](/recipes/rose-tea-liver) is built exactly around this idea.

These are gentle traditions, not prescriptions. Enjoy them as food, not as fixes.

## A small Wood ritual

When the day leaves you tight and snappy, try a five-minute pause rather than a fifth task. Step outside, stretch the sides of your ribs (where liver energy is said to pool), and let yourself *not* solve anything for a moment. Then a cup of something floral. It's a ritual of attention, not a treatment — but the Wood type who actually takes it tends to come back looser.
