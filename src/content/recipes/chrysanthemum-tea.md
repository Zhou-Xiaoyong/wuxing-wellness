There is a reason chrysanthemum tea shows up on nearly every Chinese family's shelf by late summer. It is the gentle off-switch for a body running a little hot and a head that has been staring at a screen since breakfast.

In the five-element view, chrysanthemum belongs to the Metal phase — the season of autumn, the lungs, and the kind of dry, irritable heat that builds when summer refuses to leave. Brewed, it is pale gold, faintly sweet, and clean-tasting in a way that makes you sit back in your chair without meaning to.

## Ingredients

- 8–10 dried chrysanthemum flowers (white or yellow, food-grade)
- 1 small slice dried goji berry (optional, for a touch of sweetness)
- 500 ml just-boiled water
- A few crystal rock sugar pearls (optional)

## Method

1. Rinse the dried flowers briefly in cool water to wake them up.
2. Drop them into a glass teapot or two mugs.
3. Pour over just-boiled water and cover — the flowers will float, then slowly open.
4. Steep 5 minutes. Add goji and a little rock sugar if you like it sweet.
5. Sip it warm, letting the mild, almost grassy sweetness sit on your tongue.

## Why it works

Chrysanthemum (菊花, *jú huā*) has been on the Chinese pharmacopeia for close to two thousand years. The *Compendium of Materia Medica* (《本草纲目》) notes it for calming wind and soothing the eyes — the same complaints we'd now chalk up to too much screen time and not enough blinking. In everyday terms, it is the herbal equivalent of stepping into shade after walking in the sun: it does not sedate you, it just cools the surface heat.

It pairs naturally with goji, which the same tradition links to the eyes and to moisture. Together they make a tea that feels like maintenance, not medicine.

## When to reach for it

- After a long day of emails and a forehead that feels warm.
- When your eyes are dry, itchy, or oddly sensitive to light.
- In early autumn, when the air turns dry and your throat feels scraped.
- As a calm alternative to a second coffee.

This is a *ritual of cooling*, not a treatment. If you are pregnant, on medication, or dealing with a specific health condition, check with your practitioner before making any herb a daily habit.
