If there is one "feel better" tea in the Chinese kitchen, this is it. It is what a grandmother puts in front of you when you are pale, tired, and a little cranky — and it tastes like a warm apology.

In the five-element system, goji (枸杞, *gǒu qǐ*) feeds the Wood phase: the liver, the eyes, and the deep reserve of energy the Chinese call *blood*. Red dates (红枣, *hóng zǎo*) do the steady, unglamorous work of building that same blood back up. Neither is a stimulant. Together they are closer to a slow charge than a jolt.

## Ingredients

- 1 tablespoon dried goji berries
- 4–5 red dates (jujube), pitted and torn open
- 500 ml water
- 2 slices fresh ginger (optional, for warmth)

## Method

1. Rinse the goji and red dates.
2. Tear each red date open so the flavor can get out.
3. Simmer both in the water for 10 minutes — the liquid turns a soft amber.
4. Drop in the goji for the last 2 minutes (they go bitter if boiled too long).
5. Pour into a mug. Sip warm, chewing the softened goji and date if you like.

## Why it works

The *Compendium of Materia Medica* (《本草纲目》) describes goji as nourishing the liver and kidney and "brightening the eyes" — which is exactly the traditional read on tired eyes and low reserves. Red dates have sat at the top of the Chinese pharmacopeia since *The Divine Farmer's Materia Medica* (《神农本草经》), valued for tonifying the middle and calming the spirit without any heaviness.

In plain terms: this is the tea you make when you want to feel human again, not wired.

## When to reach for it

- After a stretch of late nights or screen-heavy weeks.
- When your eyes feel dry and your energy is flat rather than anxious.
- In late winter and early spring, when Wood energy is waking up and wants fuel.
- As a gentler cousin to coffee, sipped slowly mid-morning.

It is a nourishing ritual, not a treatment. If you are pregnant, diabetic, or on medication, talk to your practitioner before making any herb a daily habit — goji can interact with a few drugs, including blood thinners.
