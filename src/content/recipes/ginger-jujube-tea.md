This is the tea Chinese medicine reaches for first when someone says "I'm always cold" — not the kind of cold you fix with a jacket, but the hollow, slow kind that starts in the stomach.

In the five-element map, the Earth phase is the center: the spleen and stomach, digestion, and the steady warmth that everything else runs on. Fresh ginger (生姜, *shēng jiāng*) is the classic "warm the middle" herb — it stokes the digestive fire without making you sweat. Red dates lend the round, grounding sweetness so the heat doesn't feel sharp.

## Ingredients

- 4–5 thin slices fresh ginger (with skin if organic)
- 4–5 red dates (jujube), pitted and torn open
- 500 ml water
- A few crystal rock sugar pearls or a drizzle of honey (optional)

## Method

1. Rinse the ginger and red dates.
2. Tear the dates open and add everything to a small pot with the water.
3. Bring to a gentle simmer and keep it there 15 minutes — the kitchen will smell like a spice market.
4. Strain into mugs. Sweeten lightly if you like.
5. Sip it hot, slowly, letting the warmth sit in your middle.

## Why it works

Ginger has been used in China for digestion and cold for over two thousand years; the *Compendium of Materia Medica* (《本草纲目》) lists it for warming the center and dispersing chill. Red dates (红枣) are the quiet partner — they tonify the same middle and calm the system so the warmth lands instead of racing off as sweat.

Think of it as turning the pilot light back on in a stove that's been left on low.

## When to reach for it

- Cold hands and feet that never quite warm up.
- A sluggish, gurgly stomach or nausea in the morning.
- The first cold snap of the season, or after too much iced anything.
- Menstrual cramps with a craving for warmth and pressure.

Skip it if you already feel hot, flushed, or "internal heat" — mouth ulcers, a red face, restless at night. This tea is for cold types. It is a warming ritual, not a treatment; check with your practitioner if you're pregnant or on medication.
