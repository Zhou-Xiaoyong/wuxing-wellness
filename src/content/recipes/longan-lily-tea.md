There is a particular kind of tired that sleep doesn't touch — the kind where you're in bed but your brain is still filing paperwork. This is the tea for that.

In the five-element system, the Fire phase rules the heart, and in Chinese medicine the heart is said to "house the spirit" (*shen*). When the heart's blood runs thin — from overwork, worry, or simply a long stretch of doing — the spirit has no soft place to land, and you lie awake. Longan (桂圆, *guì yuán*) and lily bulb (百合, *bǎi hé*) are the classic pair for exactly this: one feeds the blood, the other cools and settles the mind.

## Ingredients

- 8–10 dried longan (shelled, or use shelled + pitted)
- 10–12 dried lily bulbs (bai he)
- 3 red dates, torn open (optional)
- 500 ml water

## Method

1. Rinse the longan and lily bulbs.
2. Add them with the water to a small pot.
3. Simmer gently 15 minutes until the liquid is faintly sweet and cloudy.
4. Strain into a mug. Sip warm, an hour or so before bed.

## Why it works

Longan has been valued since *The Divine Farmer's Materia Medica* (《神农本草经》) for tonifying the heart and spleen and "calming the spirit." Lily bulb appears throughout Chinese home remedy books as the gentle moistener that takes the edge off a restless, dry, over-heated mind. Together they read less like a sedative and more like a dimmer switch.

## When to reach for it

- When you're tired but your thoughts won't slow down at night.
- After weeks of high-stakes work or constant decisions.
- During the kind of stress that lives behind your eyes.
- As a screen-free, caffeine-free evening ritual.

It is a calming ritual, not a treatment. If you have a diagnosed sleep disorder, are pregnant, or take medication, check with your practitioner before making any herb part of your routine.
